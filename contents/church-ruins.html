Budiao Ruins

History

The first settlers of the town of Daraga came from a place called Budiao. Under the leadership of Juan Salcedo, the Spaniards arrived and established this village. The name "Budiao" is derived from the word "badjao," which means "the greatest," as demonstrated by the "ilustrados" (educated elite) among the spectators of bullfights. In Budiao, there was a designated area for the battle between men and bulls, following a tradition reminiscent of ancient Rome.

For the Spaniards, watching and observing bullfights brought a unique kind of pleasure. However, in 1578, the town was re-established in Cagsawa. This relocation was due to the people's fear of another eruption from Mayon Volcano, which could destroy their properties and livelihoods. On June 12, 1772, the town was moved once again—this time farther away from the volcano. It was relocated to the top of a hill where the famous Daraga Church now stands.

On February 1, 1814, after more than 40 years of peaceful living in their new settlement, an unexpected tragedy struck. Mayon Volcano erupted, covering the entire town in ash. The volcano's unrest did not cease, so in 1993, the residents were permanently relocated to their new home in the southern barangay of Anislag.

Bull Fighting

Barangay Budiao is known for its "bullfighting," where "bullfighters" and bulls face off in a designated "bull arena" located in Barangay Budiao, Daraga, Albay.

Bullfighting is said to be one of Spain's influences on the Philippines, considered both a ceremony and a festival. Beyond that, it is a cultural symbol for the residents of Barangay Budiao, as it was once a common tradition. Because of this, it is regarded as part of the barangay's history.

Many claim that bullfighting and cockfighting are similar, but the two have significant differences. Cockfighting, or sabong, is a match between two roosters, while bullfighting is a contest where a bull is pitted against a "bullfighter" (a player usually riding a horse), also called a torero. There are three types of toreros: the matador de toros (the main fighter), the picador (two supporters of the matador positioned behind the horse), and the banderillero (three supporters of the matador stationed at the horse's feet).

The toreros begin their attack once the bull enters the arena. The fight is divided into four parts. First, the matador de toros observes the bull's movements, and the picador initiates the first strike. Second, the banderilleros stab colorful sticks with spikes into the bull's muscles to weaken its movements and prevent attacks. Next comes the "death" phase, executed by the matador de toros, who attacks the bull's aorta (main blood vessel) with a sword, potentially leading to the bull's death. Finally, the fight is judged. The Presidente (head judge) and the spectators determine the outcome.

Another difference between cockfighting and bullfighting is the prize. In cockfighting, winners receive money, while in bullfighting, the matador may be awarded the bull's ear and tail, depending on the judgment of the Presidente and the audience. Beyond physical rewards, one of the most coveted honors is recognition as a skilled bullfighter.

Because of this, bullfighting is seen as an activity contrary to the teachings of the Church, as it is considered a form of gambling. It is also said to violate Christian principles, which emphasize valuing animals as part of human responsibility. Additionally, it is viewed as a practice that leads to the "senseless" death of bulls. Despite all this, bullfighting remained a popular form of entertainment in the past. It was once recognized as a daily tradition and is now acknowledged as part of history.

The Devil in "Apay"

This is a barber’s tale that became famous in their barangay. According to them, it is about a married couple—a man and a woman—who once lived in Budiao. Their relationship turned sour due to the husband’s infidelity.

One day, the wife was searching for her husband, but the man hid among the leaves of a taro plant, locally called apay. However, his wife spotted him and began cursing and shouting, "Jusmiyong apay na an!" ("Good grief, it's the apay devil!"). From that moment on, the legend of The Devil in the Apay began.

Budiao Ruins: The Distinctive

Mayon Volcano along with the Daraga Church
The Budiao Ruins are recognized as a damaged church located in Barangay Budiao, Daraga, Albay. It is dedicated to the Assumption of Our Lady, symbolizing Budiao's history as a town.

Budiao began as a visita (mission chapel) under the parish of St. James the Great in Cagsawa, then part of the province of Camarines. In 1772, the people of Cagsawa petitioned the Superior Government in Manila to relocate the visita to Daraga due to fears of potential destruction from Mayon Volcano's eruptions. The request was approved, but the transfer did not proceed because Fermin Zaldivar, the new Alcalde Mayor of Camarines, opposed it. Instead, he advocated for moving to Budiao, leading to its establishment as a town on November 29, 1786, through a decree by the Superior Government.

At the same time, Fr. Juan Duares resisted the transfer and the leadership of the new parish. Fortunately, in 1798, Fr. Francisco Aragoes took over the administration of the Budiao and Cagsawa parish. The church was constructed using volcanic stones, with crushed shells and corals serving as mortar. However, it was destroyed by the eruption of Mayon Volcano on February 1, 1814, when pyroclastic flows devastated the church building, killing 200 people inside. This was followed by lahars and other volcanic debris that buried the structure up to the top of its walls.

In 2018, archaeological excavations revealed that the Budiao Church might have been larger than Cagsawa Church, possibly reaching a height of 10 meters. That same year, the first holy mass was held within the ruins after more than 200 years since its destruction.

In 2017, the Municipal Government of Daraga declared the Budiao Ruins as a local heritage site.

Today, the Budiao Ruins are officially recognized as the 5th National Cultural Treasure in the province of Albay.

Mount Mayon

Daraga, Albay

Mayon Volcano, located in the province of Albay in the Bicol Region of the Philippines, is renowned for its near-perfect symmetrical cone shape. Standing at 2,463 meters (8,081 feet), it is one of the most active volcanoes in the country and a prominent symbol of natural beauty and danger. Its frequent eruptions have shaped both the landscape and the lives of the people living around it, making it a vital part of Bicolano culture and identity.

Daraga Church

Sta. Maria Street, Daraga, Albay

One of the few of Albay’s heritage churches that withstood the effects of Mayon volcano’s eruptions is the Daraga Church or also known as Nuestra Señora de la Porteria Parish Church. Perched on a hill with a commanding view of the majestic Mayon Volcano, Daraga Church stands as a lasting testament of faith, artistry, and resilience of Albayanos.

Farm Plate

Gabawan, Daraga, Albay

If you think Daraga, Albay is only about historic ruins and the iconic Mayon Volcano, think again. This vibrant town is not only rich in heritage—with churches, colonial structures, and landscapes that echo centuries of Filipino history—but also brimming with modern attractions that reflect its growth, creativity, and resilience. And nestled quietly in its scenic hillsides lies one of the most delightful surprises in Bicol: FarmPlate.

Cagsawa Ruins

Barangay Busay, Daraga, Albay

The Cagsawa Ruins (also spelled as Kagsawa, historically spelled as Cagsaua) are the remnants of a 16th-century Franciscan church, the Cagsawa church. It was originally built in the town of Cagsawa in 1587 but was burned down and destroyed by Dutch pirates in 1636. It was rebuilt in 1724 by Fr. Francisco Blanco but was destroyed again, along with the town of Cagsawa, on February 1, 1814, during the eruption of Mayon Volcano.
